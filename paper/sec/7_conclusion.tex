\section{Conclusion}

We have presented a lever-based interventional attribution framework for
street-view perception that moves beyond correlational feature
importance toward testable, per-scene hypotheses. By constructing a
bounded ontology-scoped scene-specific candidate set, realising
constrained prompt-only edits, and filtering them through a validity
audit, we operationalise the question of whether a scene admits multiple
distinct \emph{single-lever} intervention options that can each be
tested independently against the original image. In the present $N{=}50$
study, 177 of 250 proposed candidates pass the validity audit, all 50
scenes admit at least one valid lever, and 48 admit multiple. The
resulting pattern is heterogeneous rather than uniform: roadway-marking
and maintenance edits are especially reliable, while environmental and
legibility levers are more mixed in both realisability and auxiliary
effect. Human pairwise evaluation remains the longer-term endpoint, but
the current paper now supports a stronger claim than a small pilot: a
bounded intervention-screening and auditing protocol can recover
multiple valid scene-specific urban perception hypotheses at scale.
