\section{Preliminary Results}
\label{sec:results}

\noindent\textbf{Dataset and setup.}
We draw our image pool from SPECS (Street Perception Evaluation
Considering Socioeconomics)~\cite{quintana2025}, a demographically
balanced perception survey in which 1{,}000 participants from five
countries rated street-view scenes on ten perceptual indicators---six
traditional (safe, lively, wealthy, beautiful, boring, depressing) and
four new (live nearby, walk, cycle, green).
SPECS provides per-image perception scores grounded in diverse human
judgements, making it a principled source for studying which visual
levers shift a specific attribute.
We sample $N{=}50$ scenes from five SPECS cities (Amsterdam, Abuja,
San Francisco, Santiago, Singapore; 10 per city), stratified jointly by
baseline safety score and visual complexity so that the pilot spans the
full range of starting conditions---from low-safety scenes where
intervention is most needed to high-safety scenes that may offer fewer
actionable levers.
Each scene allows up to $K{=}5$ lever candidates with a stochastic
budget of $T{=}5$ generation attempts per candidate.

\noindent\textbf{Validity summary.}
Of 250 proposed candidates, 177 passed the validity audit, giving an
overall valid-edit rate of $0.708$ (95\% Wilson CI $[0.649, 0.761]$).
All 50 scenes admit at least one valid lever and 48 admit multiple;
mean per-scene coverage is $|V(x)|/|L(x)| = 0.708$. Detailed failure
analysis is in Appendix~\ref{sec:validity_appendix}.

\noindent\textbf{Auxiliary directional shifts.}
Among the 177 valid edits, the auxiliary scorer produces a mean shift of
$+0.366$ (median $+0.184$, range $[-3.624, +3.842]$). Using threshold
$\theta_{\mathrm{aux}}{=}0.1$, 95 of 177 valid edits fall in
$E_{\mathrm{aux}}(x,a)$. The strongest positive examples come from
lane-marking repainting, crosswalk repainting, and localized greenery
addition. At the same time, some valid edits remain neutral or
negative, reinforcing the distinction between valid realization and
attribute improvement.

Table~\ref{tab:levers} reveals clear family-level patterns. Mobility
Infrastructure yields the strongest mean auxiliary shift
($+0.579$) despite a slightly lower valid rate than Physical
Maintenance, suggesting that road-marking edits are especially salient
to the auxiliary safety scorer once they are realised successfully.
Physical Maintenance is the most reliable family overall
($71/92$, rate $0.772$, 95\% CI $[0.676, 0.846]$), driven by consistent
success on surface cleaning, litter removal, and facade repair.
Environmental Amenity is the most heterogeneous family: localized
greenery addition is almost always valid and strongly positive, whereas
lighting repair has the weakest valid rate and tree canopy management
produces negative mean auxiliary movement. Visual Legibility remains a
small-sample family and is mixed in sign, indicating that prompt-only
editing can realise these levers but not yet with stable directional
effect.

Table~\ref{tab:cities} shows that valid-edit rates remain fairly high
across all five cities, with Amsterdam and Santiago strongest on
realisation rate and Singapore lowest. Directional auxiliary movement is
more context-dependent: Santiago and San Francisco have the largest
positive mean shifts, whereas Abuja and Singapore are near zero or
slightly negative on average. The important point is that the final
rerun removes the earlier planner/API imbalance, so these city
comparisons are no longer confounded by missing candidate generation.

\noindent\textbf{Baseline score and lever realisability.}
Figure~\ref{fig:baseline_vs_valid} plots each scene's baseline safety
score $f_a(x)$ against its valid lever count $|V(x)|$. At $N{=}50$, no
meaningful monotonic relationship is observed (Spearman
$\rho{=}0.095$, $p{=}0.511$). In other words, once generation reliability
is no longer the binding constraint, scenes with lower baseline safety
do not obviously admit more valid levers than higher-baseline scenes.
We revisit this null result in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.
