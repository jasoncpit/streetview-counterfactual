\subsection{Problem Setup}

Let $x \in \mathcal{X}$ denote a street-view image, and let
$a \in \mathcal{A}$ denote a perceptual attribute of interest, such as
safety. Our goal is not to identify arbitrary salient pixels, but to
determine which minimal plausible visual changes can be applied to $x$
in a spatially grounded and semantically interpretable way while
preserving the identity of the place.

We therefore take the explanatory atom to be a \emph{lever
intervention}, rather than a raw image region. A lever intervention is
defined as the structured tuple
\begin{equation}
  l = (c, s, d, \tau),
\end{equation}
where $c$ is a lever concept (e.g.\ greenery, graffiti, storefront
transparency), $s$ is the grounded scene support in image $x$, $d$ is
the direction of intervention (e.g.\ add, remove, increase, repair),
and $\tau$ is a constrained edit template specifying the allowable
semantic transformation.

For a given image $x$, let $L(x)$ denote the closed scene-specific
candidate set instantiated from a curated lever ontology. Here,
``closed'' means complete with respect to the chosen ontology and the
visible cues it permits, not complete with respect to all possible
perceptual mechanisms.

Our method first identifies the subset of candidate levers that can be
realized as valid accepted edits under a bounded intervention protocol:
\begin{equation}
  V(x) = \{ l_i \in L(x) \mid \exists \tilde{x}_i \text{ such that } \tilde{x}_i \text{ passes the validity audit} \}.
\end{equation}

In the full protocol, perceptual effectiveness is then estimated only
over this valid set using randomized human pairwise evaluation.
Accordingly, the set of observed effective levers is defined as
\begin{equation}
  E(x,a) = \{ l_i \in V(x) \mid p_i > \theta \},
\end{equation}
where $p_i$ is the human preference share for the accepted edit
generated from $l_i$ in a pairwise comparison against the original
image, and $\theta$ is a preregistered effectiveness threshold.

Importantly, $E(x,a)$ should be interpreted as the set of
\emph{observed effective lever interventions under bounded, valid,
single-lever edits}. We do not claim to identify intrinsic causal
effects of lever concepts in isolation, nor do we assume independence
between co-occurring levers. Interactions between levers are deferred
to future work.


\subsection{Phase 1: Scene-Specific Lever Candidate Construction}

Rather than beginning from exhaustive object segmentation, we begin from
a curated ontology of manipulable visual levers relevant to perceptual
judgments. The ontology spans interpretable domains such as material
upkeep, environmental amenity, openness/activity, and visual
order/control. Each domain contains concrete lever concepts, such as
graffiti, litter, facade repair, greenery, lighting, and storefront
transparency.

For each image $x$, we instantiate scene-specific candidates by
grounding ontology concepts to visible and editable locations in the
scene. Grounding is performed at the level of semantically interpretable
supports rather than exhaustive pixel partitioning: each candidate
specifies \emph{what} is to be changed, \emph{where} the change is
intended to occur, and \emph{how} the change may be expressed. This
yields instantiated tuples
\begin{equation}
  l_i = (c_i, s_i, d_i, \tau_i),
\end{equation}
where $s_i$ provides the intended scene support and $\tau_i$ restricts
the allowable semantic form of the intervention.

For example, a scene may instantiate candidates such as
\emph{remove graffiti from this wall},
\emph{add modest greenery near this entrance}, or
\emph{increase ground-floor transparency in this storefront}. The
resulting set $L(x)$ is therefore scene-specific, semantically
structured, and explicitly scoped by the lever ontology. If needed,
candidates may be prioritized by a proposal heuristic for computational
efficiency, but this prioritization affects only the order of testing
under a bounded budget and is not part of the estimand.

\subsection{Phase 2: Bounded Stochastic Lever Intervention}

For each candidate lever intervention $l_i = (c_i,s_i,d_i,\tau_i)$, we
construct a constrained edit instruction and apply a black-box
prompt-only image generator $G$ to produce a candidate counterfactual:
\begin{equation}
  x'_{i,j} = G(x;\, c_i, s_i, d_i, \tau_i, \varphi_j),
\end{equation}
where $\varphi_j$ denotes stochastic generation settings such as random
seed or guidance parameters, and $j$ indexes repeated attempts under a
bounded retry budget. In the current implementation, $G$ is
instantiated using \texttt{flux-kontext-max}, but the framework is
generator-agnostic.

Because prompt-only generations may alter scene identity or introduce
non-local and bundled changes, each candidate is subjected to a
validity audit before it can be retained as a tested intervention.
Given the source image $x$, generated candidate $x'_{i,j}$, and
intended lever specification $(c_i,s_i,d_i,\tau_i)$, the audit
evaluates four conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{same-place preservation}: the edited image still depicts
  the same underlying location;
  \item \textbf{locality}: changes remain substantially confined to the
  intended support $s_i$;
  \item \textbf{realism}: the edited image is visually coherent and
  photorealistic;
  \item \textbf{plausibility}: the edit represents a credible instance
  of the intended lever intervention rather than an arbitrary or bundled
  transformation.
\end{enumerate}

If at least one candidate passes within a retry budget of $T$ attempts,
the first accepted edit $\tilde{x}_i$ is retained as the realized
intervention corresponding to $l_i$, and the lever is included in
$V(x)$. If no candidate passes, the lever is recorded as unrealized
under the current intervention protocol.

We report \emph{valid-edit coverage} explicitly as
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Coverage}(x) = \frac{|V(x)|}{|L(x)|},
\end{equation}
since failure to realize a valid edit is itself informative about the
practical limits of current generative intervention methods.

\subsection{Phase 3: Human Perceptual Evaluation}

In the full protocol, for each accepted edit $\tilde{x}_i$ generated
from lever intervention $l_i \in V(x)$, we estimate directional
perceptual effect using randomized human pairwise judgments. Let $p_i$
denote the proportion of raters who judge the edited image to exhibit a
higher level of attribute $a$ than the original image. A lever
intervention is considered observed effective when its preference share
exceeds the preregistered threshold:
\begin{equation}
  l_i \in E(x,a)
  \iff
  p_i > \theta.
\end{equation}

This stage therefore evaluates perceptual effectiveness only over valid
realized interventions, rather than conflating editing success with
perceptual impact.

\subsection{Auxiliary Classifier Scoring}

For pilot analysis and candidate prioritization, we also measure the
directional attribute shift using a pretrained perception classifier
$f_a$:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_i = f_a(\tilde{x}_i) - f_a(x).
\end{equation}
This score is treated strictly as an auxiliary proxy for perceptual
change. It is not the basis of the estimand and is reported only to
summarize pilot behavior before the human study is complete. In the
current pilot, we therefore defer human evaluation and report auxiliary
classifier shifts only as a provisional analysis.



\subsection{Interpretation}

Our method should be understood as \emph{constrained interventional
search over scene-specific lever hypotheses}. It does not claim
pixel-level decomposition, exhaustive recovery of all perceptual cues, or
full causal identification. Instead, it identifies semantically
interpretable, spatially grounded lever interventions that can be
realized validly and that produce human-measured directional preference
shifts under single-lever testing.

This framing is particularly suited to questions of the form:
\emph{what minimal plausible change would make this place feel safer?}
