\subsection{Problem Setup}

Let $x \in \mathcal{X}$ denote a street-view image, and let
$a \in \mathcal{A}$ denote a perceptual attribute, such as safety.
Our goal is not to identify arbitrary salient pixels, but to discover
which \emph{minimal plausible visual change} would increase perceived
attribute $a$ while preserving the identity of the place.

We therefore treat the explanatory atom as a \emph{lever intervention},
rather than a raw image region. A lever intervention is defined as the
structured tuple
\begin{equation}
  l = (c, s, d, \tau),
\end{equation}
where $c$ is a lever concept (e.g.\ greenery, graffiti, storefront
transparency), $s$ is the spatial support in image $x$, $d$ is the
direction of intervention (e.g.\ add, remove, increase, repair), and
$\tau$ is a constrained edit template specifying the allowable semantic
transformation.

For a given image $x$, let $\mathcal{L}(x)$ denote the closed
scene-specific candidate set instantiated from a curated lever ontology.
Here, ``closed'' means complete with respect to the chosen ontology and
the visible cues it permits, not complete with respect to all possible
perceptual mechanisms. We seek to identify the set of \emph{observed
effective levers} under our intervention protocol:
\begin{equation}
  E(x,a) = \{ l_i \in \mathcal{L}(x) \mid l_i \in V \;\wedge\; p_i > \theta \},
\end{equation}
where $V$ is the set of accepted lever edits, $p_i$ is the human
preference share for the accepted edit generated from $l_i$ in a
pairwise safety comparison against the original image, and $\theta$ is a
preregistered human preference threshold.

Importantly, $E(x,a)$ should be interpreted as the set of
\emph{observed effective lever interventions under bounded valid
single-lever edits}. We do not claim to identify intrinsic causal
effects of lever concepts in isolation, nor do we assume independence
between co-occurring levers. Interactions between levers are deferred to
future work.


\subsection{Phase 1: Scene-Specific Lever Candidate Construction}

Rather than beginning from exhaustive object segmentation, we begin from
a curated ontology of manipulable visual levers relevant to perceptual
judgments. The ontology spans interpretable domains such as material
upkeep, environmental amenity, openness/activity, and visual
order/control. Each domain contains concrete lever concepts, such as
graffiti, litter, facade repair, greenery, lighting, and storefront
transparency.

For each image $x$, we instantiate scene-specific candidates by
grounding these lever concepts to visible locations in the scene. Each
instantiated candidate yields a tuple
$l_i = (c_i, s_i, d_i, \tau_i)$,
where $s_i$ provides the editable spatial support and $\tau_i$
restricts the semantic form of the intervention. For example, a scene
may instantiate candidates such as
\emph{remove graffiti from this wall},
\emph{add modest greenery near this entrance}, or
\emph{increase ground-floor transparency in this storefront}.

This stage produces the candidate set $\mathcal{L}(x)$.
If needed, candidates may be ranked for computational efficiency using a
proposal heuristic, but this ranking is only used to prioritize testing
under a bounded budget. It is not part of the estimand and does not
determine success.

\subsection{Phase 2: Bounded Stochastic Lever Intervention}

For each candidate lever intervention $l_i = (c_i,s_i,d_i,\tau_i)$, we
construct a constrained edit instruction and apply a black-box
prompt-only image generator $G$ to produce a candidate counterfactual:
\begin{equation}
  x'_{i,j} = G(x;\, c_i, s_i, d_i, \tau_i, \varphi_j),
\end{equation}
where $\varphi_j$ denotes stochastic generation settings such as random
seed or guidance parameters, and $j$ indexes retry attempts. In the
current implementation, $G$ is instantiated by
\texttt{flux-kontext-max}, but the framework is generator-agnostic.

Because prompt-only generations may alter scene identity or introduce
non-local and bundled changes, each candidate is filtered through a
validity audit before it is accepted as a tested intervention. Given the
source image $x$, generated candidate $x'_{i,j}$, and intended lever
specification $(c_i,s_i,d_i,\tau_i)$, the audit checks four validity
conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{same-place preservation}: the edited image still depicts
  the same underlying location;
  \item \textbf{locality}: changes remain substantially confined to the
  intended spatial support $s_i$;
  \item \textbf{realism}: the edited image is visually coherent and
  photorealistic;
  \item \textbf{plausibility}: the edit represents a credible instance
  of the intended lever intervention rather than an arbitrary or bundled
  transformation.
\end{enumerate}

If at least one candidate passes within a retry budget of $T$ attempts,
the first accepted edit is retained:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{x}_i \in V
  \quad \text{for} \quad
  l_i \in \mathcal{L}(x),
\end{equation}
where $V$ denotes the set of accepted lever edits. If no candidate
passes, the lever is recorded as invalid under the intervention
protocol.

We report \emph{valid-edit coverage} explicitly as
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Coverage}(x) = \frac{|V|}{|\mathcal{L}(x)|},
\end{equation}
since inability to realize a valid edit is itself informative about the
limits of current generative intervention methods.

\subsection{Phase 3: Human Perceptual Evaluation}

For each accepted edit $\tilde{x}_i \in V$ generated from lever
intervention $l_i$, we estimate directional effect using randomized
human pairwise judgments. Let $p_i$ denote the proportion of raters who
judge the edited image to look safer than the original image for
attribute $a$. A lever intervention is considered \emph{observed
effective} when its preference share exceeds the preregistered
threshold:
\begin{equation}
  l_i \in E(x,a)
  \iff
  p_i > \theta.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Auxiliary Classifier Scoring}

For pilot analysis and candidate prioritization, we also measure the
directional attribute shift using a pretrained perception classifier
$f_a$:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_i = f_a(\tilde{x}_i) - f_a(x).
\end{equation}
This score is treated strictly as an auxiliary proxy for perceptual
change. It is not the basis of the estimand and is reported only to
summarize pilot behavior before the human study is complete.



\subsection{Interpretation}

Our method should be understood as \emph{constrained interventional
search over scene-specific lever hypotheses}. It does not claim
pixel-level decomposition, exhaustive recovery of all perceptual cues, or
full causal identification. Instead, it identifies semantically
interpretable, spatially grounded lever interventions that can be
realized validly and that produce human-measured directional preference
shifts under single-lever testing.

This framing is particularly suited to questions of the form:
\emph{what minimal plausible change would make this place feel safer?}
