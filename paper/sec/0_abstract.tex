\begin{abstract}
Street-view perception models can predict subjective attributes such as safety and wealth at scale, yet they remain fundamentally correlational: they do not answer the scene-level question practitioners care about most --- which visual changes would plausibly shift human perception for this specific street? Drawing on urban design theory that treats perceived safety as the product of multiple co-occurring visual cues, we ask not only which localized urban cues matter, but whether individual scenes admit multiple substitutable visual pathways to the same perceptual outcome. We propose a lever-based interventional attribution framework that reframes scene-level explainability as a structured search over manipulable urban cues. Rather than treating arbitrary image regions as the explanatory unit, we define structured lever interventions with a semantic concept, spatial support, intervention direction, and constrained edit template. Candidate interventions are generated via prompt-conditioned diffusion editing and validated by a VLM-as-judge critic for same-place preservation, locality, realism, and plausibility. On a pilot of N=100 street-view images spanning five cities, we show that different lever families exhibit distinct realization and auxiliary shift profiles, and that some scenes admit multiple valid levers.
\end{abstract}
