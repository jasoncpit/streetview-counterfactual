\begin{abstract}
    Street-view perception models predict subjective attributes such as safety at scale, yet remain correlational: they do not reveal which localized visual changes would plausibly shift human judgement for a specific scene. We propose a lever-based interventional attribution framework that recasts scene-level explainability as a bounded search over structured counterfactual edits. Each candidate lever specifies a semantic concept, spatial support, intervention direction, and constrained edit template, drawn from a theory-grounded intervention vocabulary organised into four families: Physical Maintenance, Environmental Amenity, Visual Legibility, and Mobility Infrastructure. Candidate edits are generated through prompt-conditioned image editing and retained only if they pass validity checks for same-place preservation, locality, realism, and plausibility. In a pilot across 50 scenes from five cities, the framework reveals preliminary proxy-based directional patterns and a practical failure taxonomy under prompt-only editing. Mobility Infrastructure and Physical Maintenance levers show the most promising auxiliary safety shifts in the current setup, while planner triage, generator alignment, critic calibration, and human evaluation remain key next steps. Human pairwise safety judgements remain the ground-truth endpoint; the present work introduces the protocol and pilot baseline needed to study that evaluation systematically.
\end{abstract}
